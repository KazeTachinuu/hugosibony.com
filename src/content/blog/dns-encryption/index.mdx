Classic DNS (Do53) is cleartext on port 53: your ISP reads, and can rewrite, every name you look up. Three encrypted transports fix that, to different degrees. For the lookup this protects, start with [how DNS works](/blog/what-is-dns/).

<Callout type="note" title="How DoH encrypts">
No new crypto. The query is the same DNS bytes, sent as an HTTP POST (`application/dns-message`) inside a normal **TLS** connection on port 443. The answer comes back in the response body. Ordinary DNS, over HTTPS.
</Callout>

The catch in every encrypted mode: the observer still sees the resolver's IP, and the TLS **SNI** can reveal which resolver you picked unless Encrypted Client Hello (ECH) hides it. Your individual queries stay private either way.

To turn encryption on and prove it works, see [which resolver to choose](/blog/public-dns-resolvers/).

## References

- [RFC 7858](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc7858): DNS over TLS (DoT), TCP 853.
- [RFC 8484](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc8484): DNS over HTTPS (DoH), `application/dns-message` over TCP 443.
- [RFC 9250](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9250): DNS over QUIC (DoQ), UDP 853.
- [RFC 1035](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc1035): classic DNS (Do53) over UDP/TCP 53.
- [RFC 9460](https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc9460): the HTTPS record and its Encrypted Client Hello (ECH) hint.
